Getting Started with Diffusion Models in Computer Vision

📅 October 15, 2025 Machine Learning Computer Vision

Diffusion models have revolutionized the field of generative AI in recent years, particularly in computer vision tasks. From text-to-image generation to image editing, these models have shown remarkable capabilities. In this post, I'll share my journey learning about diffusion models and implementing them in my research.

What Are Diffusion Models?

At their core, diffusion models work by gradually adding noise to data and then learning to reverse this process. The forward process adds Gaussian noise over many timesteps, while the reverse process learns to denoise and reconstruct the original data. This simple yet powerful idea has led to state-of-the-art results in image generation.

Key Concepts I Found Helpful

  • Forward Diffusion Process: Gradually adds noise to images following a schedule
  • Reverse Process: A neural network learns to predict and remove the noise
  • Score Matching: Training the model to estimate the gradient of the data distribution
  • Sampling: Generating new images by iteratively denoising random noise

My Implementation Experience

When I first started implementing a diffusion model using PyTorch, I was surprised by how conceptually simple the training loop is. The main challenge was understanding the math behind the noise schedule and variance calculations. Here's a simplified version of the core training step: 

# Simplified diffusion training step
t = torch.randint(0, num_timesteps, (batch_size,))
noise = torch.randn_like(x)
x_noisy = sqrt_alpha[t] * x + sqrt_one_minus_alpha[t] * noise
predicted_noise = model(x_noisy, t)
loss = F.mse_loss(predicted_noise, noise)

Applications in My Research

In my current project on tunnel lining water leakage segmentation, I'm exploring how diffusion models can be used for data augmentation. By generating synthetic training examples, we can potentially improve model robustness when real training data is limited.

Resources I Found Useful

  • The original DDPM paper by Ho et al.
  • Lilian Weng's excellent blog post on diffusion models
  • Hugging Face's diffusers library documentation
  • Various implementation tutorials on GitHub

Final Thoughts

Diffusion models represent an exciting frontier in generative AI. While they can be computationally expensive to train and sample from, recent work on faster sampling methods (like DDIM) is making them more practical. I'm excited to continue exploring their applications in my research and see where this technology goes next.

If you're interested in learning more about diffusion models or have questions about my research, feel free to reach out!

Lessons from My First Research Paper

📅 September 28, 2025 Research Personal

Getting my first paper accepted to ICONIP 2025 was an incredible milestone in my academic journey. But the path to publication was filled with valuable lessons that I wish I had known earlier. Here's what I learned along the way.

Lesson 1: Start Writing Early

One of my biggest mistakes was waiting until all experiments were "finished" before starting to write. I learned that writing is actually part of the research process—it helps clarify your thinking, identify gaps in your experiments, and communicate your ideas more effectively. Now I start drafting the introduction and related work sections as soon as I have a clear research question.

Lesson 2: Collaboration Is Key

Working with my co-authors taught me the importance of clear communication and dividing responsibilities. Regular meetings, shared documents, and being open to feedback made the process much smoother. Having different perspectives also significantly improved the quality of our work.

Lesson 3: Handle Rejection Gracefully

Before getting accepted at ICONIP, we received reviews from another conference that were quite critical. Initially, it was discouraging, but the feedback helped us identify real weaknesses in our work. We revised the paper substantially based on those comments, and the final version was much stronger.

Lesson 4: The Devil Is in the Details

Small things matter more than I expected:

  • Clear, informative figure captions
  • Consistent notation throughout the paper
  • Properly formatted references
  • Thorough proofreading (I found embarrassing typos even after multiple readings)

Lesson 5: Code and Reproducibility

Keeping clean, well-documented code from the start would have saved me countless hours. When it came time to respond to reviewer questions about implementation details, I had to dig through messy experiment scripts. Now I use version control properly and document as I go.

Looking Forward

These lessons have already proven valuable as I work on my next papers. Research is a learning process, and each paper teaches you something new about the craft of scientific writing and communication. I'm grateful for the mentorship I received and excited to keep improving.

Why I'm Pursuing a PhD in Computer Science

📅 August 12, 2025 PhD Career

As I prepare my PhD applications, I've spent a lot of time reflecting on why I want to pursue graduate school. It's a significant commitment, and I want to make sure I'm doing it for the right reasons. Here are my thoughts on why research is the right path for me.

The Joy of Discovery

What excites me most about research is the opportunity to push the boundaries of what we know. Whether it's developing new algorithms, finding better ways to solve existing problems, or discovering unexpected patterns in data—there's something deeply satisfying about contributing new knowledge to the field.

Deep Dive into Problems

In industry, there's often pressure to move quickly and ship products. While I respect that urgency and have learned a lot from project work, I crave the opportunity to deeply understand problems. A PhD offers the time and environment to really dig into complex questions without rushing to immediate applications.

Impact Through Automation

My research interests center around automation—particularly in using computer vision and machine learning to automate tasks that currently require significant human effort. I believe this work can have real-world impact, whether it's in infrastructure monitoring, medical imaging, or other domains. A PhD will give me the skills and credibility to work on these impactful problems.

The Research Community

I've been inspired by the collaborative nature of academic research. Conferences, seminars, and open-source projects create a community where people freely share ideas and build on each other's work. I want to be part of this community and contribute to the collective advancement of the field.

What I'm Looking For

In choosing PhD programs, I'm looking for:

  • Strong research groups in computer vision and machine learning
  • Advisors whose work aligns with my interests in automation and practical applications
  • Collaborative environments where I can learn from peers
  • Opportunities to work on both theoretical understanding and real-world problems

Acknowledging the Challenges

I'm not naive about the challenges of a PhD—the long hours, the inevitable setbacks, the pressure to publish. But I've experienced glimpses of these challenges during my undergraduate research, and rather than discouraging me, they've confirmed that this is work I find meaningful and want to pursue at a deeper level.

Closing Thoughts

Ultimately, I'm pursuing a PhD because I can't imagine doing anything else right now. The questions that keep me up at night are research questions. The work that makes me lose track of time is implementing and experimenting with new ideas. If that resonates with you too, maybe we'll cross paths at a conference or as collaborators someday.
